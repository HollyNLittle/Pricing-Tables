The Formation of a Software Developer

How I ended up pursuing a profession that I never even considered...

Growing up with a father in the burgeoning computer programming field, I remember computer related parts, books and printers as being ubiquitous in our home.

The memories of the noise of the printers, the green and white striped paper, floppy discs, hard discs, are especially strong. I watched the book titles and job titles as they changed over the years; COBOL, networking, systems engineer and so on.

Shortly after graduating from Miami University with a Bachelor's Degree of Fine Arts and a major in Studio Arts and Printmaking, I was living at home, working in the education field and raising my son. My father, who had long complained around the dinner table of frustration in what he found to be a dearth of good, knowledgeable, computer programming professionals to hire to complete different projects, brought an O'Reilly book on Java programming to me, directing me to learn how to program so I could help him on his current project.

Without so much as questioning the rather abrupt request to learn how to program, I delved in intent on helping and being sure to in now way let my dad down. I fell in love with O'Reilly, turning to other publishers as needed but I still recommend Head First series. After I had been programming for some time my father said that the company needed a website and directed me to create one. Again, turning to books, I delved into HTML, XHTML, CSS and Photoshop eventually building that website, taking and passing the test to become an Adobe Certification Expert and then teaching Photoshop to others at the local community college.

Once, my father's work was done at that particular company he encouraged me to take the Java certification exam and take on a job search as a computer programmer. However, in spite of his encouragement and my successfully utilizing the loops, arrays, garbage collection and so forth that I had intently studied I was not ready to embark on a job search in the field, feeling no confidence in my skill set as I had no certificate, degree or experience working for a person that wasn't a relative. I continued in the education field as the art teacher at a daycare and later teaching and tutoring at the Lorain Juvenile Detention Facility, followed by work as a Resolution Specialist first at Nissan and then at Progressive where I am currently.

Also, during this time I raised two sons and sent my oldest son off to college. I was considering my next steps careerwise, the possibility of pursuing a master's degree in business or continuing to work my way up in my current work area, when I heard an advertisement for We Can Code IT that I had already heard many times. For some reason, this particular morning upon hearing the ad I determined that I would pursue that path. Now, I am well into the program, have met new people, learned more in a short period of time than I would have thought possible and successfully completed projects using a myriad of programs and languages. The experience has been rewarding, challenging, fun and frustrating all at once, but I am so glad that I decided to walk down this path and am now tentatively making contacts and preparing to start my job search in earnest.

When I explained to my manager that I was embarking on this journey, her response was enthusiastic support and she commented that I was brave. I encourage anyone reading this that has considered pursuing a similar life change, to go for it. After some years in the workforce, I have come to realize that the primary difference between those that do and the rest of us, is neither an expensive degree nor a brilliant mind, but a belief they can.

Thank you for reading, I hope my story helps anyone of you that have dealt with a similar situation, "imposter syndrome", those of you considering or in the process of a mid-life switch in careers.
